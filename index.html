About

Where it started

I grew up in Bulawayo, Zimbabwe, where learning was something you did because you had to. It was a way of getting somewhere safer and more secure. It wasn't until college that I experienced what it felt like to study something purely because it fascinated me. That shift, from learning to survive to learning because I wanted to, changed the trajectory of everything that came after.

The Path

Finding my place in science

As a neuroscience major at Brandeis University, I joined Avi Rodal's lab studying the TDP-43 protein and its role in the neurodegenerative disorder, Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis. I was captivated by the idea that you could take the most complex system we know of — the brain — and dissect it piece by piece to understand how it functions and what happens when that function goes wrong.

I carried that curiosity to Rockefeller University, fully intending to join another neuroscience lab. But a rotation in a host-pathogen lab, just for fun, just to see, pulled me sideways into an entirely new field of biology. I landed in Luciano Marraffini's lab studying CRISPR-Cas immunity and how this systems enables bacteria to defend themselves against bacteriophages. I discovered what scientists before me discovered centuries ago: that phage are the most fulfilling system to dissect basic biology because they are simple enough to pry apart mechanistic detail in a way you simply can't in other models. That work became my PhD thesis and a first-author paper in Cell Host & Microbe.

What I took away from my time in the Marraffini Lab, though, wasn't just a thesis, but a lesson about environment.

The environment matters more than the question.

Science can be deeply impactful, but you need safety and curiosity built in together: room to fail and still show up the next day without feeling like a disappointment. That's the single most important thing to know about how I work, teach, and mentor.

That lesson became a practice when I started mentoring graduate students through RockEdu, watching someone go from stuck to curious again — and realizing how much I love to teach.

Next, I'm joining the Jackson lab at Utah State University to continue studying CRISPR-Cas systems and bacteriophage, while also teaching biochemistry — a step toward my long-term goal of a teaching-focused faculty position where I get to keep doing both.

Teaching

You already belong here

In my classroom, the focus is curiosity: building an environment safe enough that students feel free to wonder out loud, get something wrong, and stay curious anyway.

So many students walk in already convinced they're behind their peers, certain they don't quite belong in science. A good part of my teaching is about eradicating that doubt — helping each one see that they do have a place here, and that every person in the room has something real to contribute.

I've designed and taught microbiology and plant biology labs for high school students, mentored graduate students through RockEdu, and built course materials that treat curiosity and belonging as the real curriculum — not an afterthought to content.
